Mathematical Induction and Binomial Theorem

Unit 7: Mathematical Induction and Binomial Theorem ‚Äì Complete Solved Notes

üìö Core Definitions

  • Mathematical Induction: A method of mathematical proof typically used to establish that a given statement is true for all natural numbers.
  • Principle of Mathematical Induction: If \( P(n) \) is a proposition about a positive integer \( n \) such that \( P(1) \) is true, and \( P(k) \) implies \( P(k+1) \), then \( P(n) \) is true for all positive integers \( n \).
  • Modified Principle of Mathematical Induction: If \( P(n) \) is a proposition about a positive integer \( n \) such that \( P(i) \) is true for some positive integer \( i \), and \( P(k) \) implies \( P(k+1) \), then \( P(n) \) is true for all \( n \geq i \).
  • Binomial Expression: An expression consisting of two terms connected by a +ve or -ve sign, e.g., \( a + b \), \( 2x - 3y \).
  • Binomial Theorem: A theorem that describes the algebraic expansion of powers of a binomial, given by \((a + b)^n = \sum_{r=0}^n \binom{n}{r} a^{n-r} b^r\).
  • Pascal's Triangle: A triangular array of the binomial coefficients, where each number is the sum of the two directly above it.
  • Binomial Coefficients: The coefficients of the terms in the expansion of a binomial, denoted as \(\binom{n}{r}\).
  • General Term: In the expansion of \((x + y)^n\), the general term is given by \( T_{r+1} = \binom{n}{r} x^{n-r} y^r \).
  • Middle Term: In the expansion of \((x + y)^n\), if \( n \) is even, the middle term is the \(\left(\frac{n}{2} + 1\right)\)th term; if \( n \) is odd, the middle terms are the \(\left(\frac{n+1}{2}\right)\)th and \(\left(\frac{n+3}{2}\right)\)th terms.
  • Binomial Series: The infinite series expansion of \((1 + x)^n\) for rational \( n \), valid for \(|x| < 1\).

Q1(i). Prove that \( 1 + 3 + 5 + \dots + (2n - 1) = n^2 \) for every positive integer \( n \).

Solution:

Base Case: For \( n = 1 \), LHS = \( 1 \) and RHS = \( 1^2 = 1 \). So, the statement is true for \( n = 1 \).

Inductive Step: Assume the statement is true for \( n = k \), i.e., \( 1 + 3 + 5 + \dots + (2k - 1) = k^2 \).

For \( n = k + 1 \), LHS = \( 1 + 3 + 5 + \dots + (2k - 1) + (2(k+1) - 1) = k^2 + (2k + 1) = (k + 1)^2 \).

Thus, the statement is true for \( n = k + 1 \).

By the principle of mathematical induction, the statement is true for all positive integers \( n \).

‚úÖ Answer: \(\boxed{n^2}\)

Q1(ii). Prove that \( 3 + 6 + 9 + \dots + 3n = \frac{3}{2}n(n + 1) \) for every positive integer \( n \).

Solution:

Base Case: For \( n = 1 \), LHS = \( 3 \) and RHS = \( \frac{3}{2} \cdot 1 \cdot 2 = 3 \). So, the statement is true for \( n = 1 \).

Inductive Step: Assume the statement is true for \( n = k \), i.e., \( 3 + 6 + 9 + \dots + 3k = \frac{3}{2}k(k + 1) \).

For \( n = k + 1 \), LHS = \( 3 + 6 + 9 + \dots + 3k + 3(k+1) = \frac{3}{2}k(k + 1) + 3(k + 1) = \frac{3}{2}(k + 1)(k + 2) \).

Thus, the statement is true for \( n = k + 1 \).

By the principle of mathematical induction, the statement is true for all positive integers \( n \).

‚úÖ Answer: \(\boxed{\frac{3}{2}n(n + 1)}\)

Q1(iii). Prove that \( 1 + 4 + 7 + \dots + (3n - 2) = \frac{n(3n - 1)}{2} \) for every positive integer \( n \).

Solution:

Base Case: For \( n = 1 \), LHS = \( 1 \) and RHS = \( \frac{1(3 \cdot 1 - 1)}{2} = 1 \). So, the statement is true for \( n = 1 \).

Inductive Step: Assume the statement is true for \( n = k \), i.e., \( 1 + 4 + 7 + \dots + (3k - 2) = \frac{k(3k - 1)}{2} \).

For \( n = k + 1 \), LHS = \( 1 + 4 + 7 + \dots + (3k - 2) + (3(k+1) - 2) = \frac{k(3k - 1)}{2} + (3k + 1) = \frac{(k + 1)(3(k + 1) - 1)}{2} \).

Thus, the statement is true for \( n = k + 1 \).

By the principle of mathematical induction, the statement is true for all positive integers \( n \).

‚úÖ Answer: \(\boxed{\frac{n(3n - 1)}{2}}\)

Q1(iv). Prove that \( 1 + \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{4} + \dots + \frac{1}{2^{n-1}} = 2(1 - \frac{1}{2^n}) \) for every positive integer \( n \).

Solution:

Base Case: For \( n = 1 \), LHS = \( 1 \) and RHS = \( 2(1 - \frac{1}{2}) = 1 \). So, the statement is true for \( n = 1 \).

Inductive Step: Assume the statement is true for \( n = k \), i.e., \( 1 + \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{4} + \dots + \frac{1}{2^{k-1}} = 2(1 - \frac{1}{2^k}) \).

For \( n = k + 1 \), LHS = \( 1 + \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{4} + \dots + \frac{1}{2^{k-1}} + \frac{1}{2^k} = 2(1 - \frac{1}{2^k}) + \frac{1}{2^k} = 2(1 - \frac{1}{2^{k+1}}) \).

Thus, the statement is true for \( n = k + 1 \).

By the principle of mathematical induction, the statement is true for all positive integers \( n \).

‚úÖ Answer: \(\boxed{2(1 - \frac{1}{2^n})}\)

By the principle of mathematical induction, the statement is true for all positive integers \( n \).

Q1(vii). Prove that \( 2^2 + 4^2 + 6^2 + \dots + (2n)^2 = \frac{2}{3}n(n + 1)(2n + 1) \) for every positive integer \( n \).

Solution:

Base Case: For \( n = 1 \), LHS = \( 4 \) and RHS = \( \frac{2}{3} \cdot 1 \cdot 2 \cdot 3 = 4 \). So, the statement is true for \( n = 1 \).

Inductive Step: Assume the statement is true for \( n = k \), i.e., \( 2^2 + 4^2 + 6^2 + \dots + (2k)^2 = \frac{2}{3}k(k + 1)(2k + 1) \).

For \( n = k + 1 \), LHS = \( 2^2 + 4^2 + 6^2 + \dots + (2k)^2 + (2(k+1))^2 = \frac{2}{3}k(k + 1)(2k + 1) + 4(k + 1)^2 = \frac{2}{3}(k + 1)(k + 2)(2(k + 1) + 1) \).

Thus, the statement is true for \( n = k + 1 \).

By the principle of mathematical induction, the statement is true for all positive integers \( n \).

Q1(viii). Prove that \( 1 \cdot 3 + 2 \cdot 4 + 3 \cdot 5 + \dots + n(n + 2) = \frac{1}{6}n(n + 1)(2n + 7) \) for every positive integer \( n \).

Solution:

Base Case: For \( n = 1 \), LHS = \( 3 \) and RHS = \( \frac{1}{6} \cdot 1 \cdot 2 \cdot 9 = 3 \). So, the statement is true for \( n = 1 \).

Inductive Step: Assume the statement is true for \( n = k \), i.e., \( 1 \cdot 3 + 2 \cdot 4 + 3 \cdot 5 + \dots + k(k + 2) = \frac{1}{6}k(k + 1)(2k + 7) \).

For \( n = k + 1 \), LHS = \( 1 \cdot 3 + 2 \cdot 4 + 3 \cdot 5 + \dots + k(k + 2) + (k + 1)(k + 3) = \frac{1}{6}k(k + 1)(2k + 7) + (k + 1)(k + 3) = \frac{1}{6}(k + 1)(k + 2)(2(k + 1) + 7) \).

Thus, the statement is true for \( n = k + 1 \).

By the principle of mathematical induction, the statement is true for all positive integers \( n \).

‚úÖ Answer: \(\boxed{\frac{1}{6}n(n + 1)(2n + 7)}\)

Q1(ix). Prove that \( \frac{1}{1 \cdot 2} + \frac{1}{2 \cdot 3} + \frac{1}{3 \cdot 4} + \dots + \frac{1}{n(n+1)} = \frac{n}{n + 1} \) for every positive integer \( n \).

Solution:

Base Case: For \( n = 1 \), LHS = \( \frac{1}{2} \) and RHS = \( \frac{1}{2} \). So, the statement is true for \( n = 1 \).

Inductive Step: Assume the statement is true for \( n = k \), i.e., \( \frac{1}{1 \cdot 2} + \frac{1}{2 \cdot 3} + \frac{1}{3 \cdot 4} + \dots + \frac{1}{k(k+1)} = \frac{k}{k + 1} \).

For \( n = k + 1 \), LHS = \( \frac{1}{1 \cdot 2} + \frac{1}{2 \cdot 3} + \frac{1}{3 \cdot 4} + \dots + \frac{1}{k(k+1)} + \frac{1}{(k+1)(k+2)} = \frac{k}{k + 1} + \frac{1}{(k+1)(k+2)} = \frac{k+1}{k+2} \).

Thus, the statement is true for \( n = k + 1 \).

By the principle of mathematical induction, the statement is true for all positive integers \( n \).

‚úÖ Answer: \(\boxed{\frac{n}{n + 1}}\)

Q2(i). Prove that \( 2^{3n+2} - 28n - 4 \) is divisible by 49 for all \( n \in \mathbb{N} \).

Solution:

Base Case: For \( n = 1 \), \( 2^{5} - 28 \cdot 1 - 4 = 32 - 28 - 4 = 0 \), which is divisible by 49.

Inductive Step: Assume the statement is true for \( n = k \), i.e., \( 2^{3k+2} - 28k - 4 \) is divisible by 49.

For \( n = k + 1 \), \( 2^{3(k+1)+2} - 28(k+1) - 4 = 8 \cdot 2^{3k+2} - 28k - 28 - 4 = 8 \cdot 2^{3k+2} - 28k - 32 \).

This can be written as \( 8(2^{3k+2} - 28k - 4) + 49 \cdot 8k + 49 \cdot 4 \), which is divisible by 49.

Thus, the statement is true for \( n = k + 1 \).

By the principle of mathematical induction, the statement is true for all \( n \in \mathbb{N} \).

‚úÖ Answer: \(\boxed{\text{Divisible by 49}}\)

This can be written as \( 9(3^{2k+2} - 8k - 9) + 64k + 64 \), which is divisible by 64.

Thus, the statement is true for \( n = k + 1 \).

By the principle of mathematical induction, the statement is true for all \( n \in \mathbb{N} \).

‚úÖ Answer: \(\boxed{\text{Divisible by 64}}\)

Q2(iii). Prove that \( 7^n - 4^n \) is divisible by 3 for all \( n \in \mathbb{N} \).

Solution:

Base Case: For \( n = 1 \), \( 7^1 - 4^1 = 3 \), which is divisible by 3.

Inductive Step: Assume the statement is true for \( n = k \), i.e., \( 7^k - 4^k \) is divisible by 3.

For \( n = k + 1 \), \( 7^{k+1} - 4^{k+1} = 7 \cdot 7^k - 4 \cdot 4^k = 7(7^k - 4^k) + 3 \cdot 4^k \), which is divisible by 3.

Thus, the statement is true for \( n = k + 1 \).

By the principle of mathematical induction, the statement is true for all \( n \in \mathbb{N} \).

Q3(i). Prove that \( 2^{n+1} > (2n + 3) \) for all integral values of \( n \geq 2 \).

Solution:

Base Case: For \( n = 2 \), \( 2^{3} = 8 > 7 \). So, the statement is true for \( n = 2 \).

Inductive Step: Assume the statement is true for \( n = k \), i.e., \( 2^{k+1} > (2k + 3) \).

For \( n = k + 1 \), \( 2^{(k+1)+1} = 2 \cdot 2^{k+1} > 2(2k + 3) = 4k + 6 \). Since \( 4k + 6 > 2(k+1) + 3 \), the statement is true for \( n = k + 1 \).

By the principle of mathematical induction, the statement is true for all \( n \geq 2 \).

‚úÖ Answer: \(\boxed{2^{n+1} > (2n + 3)}\)

Q3(ii). Prove that \( 3^{n-1} > 2^n \) for all integral values of \( n \geq 3 \).

Solution:

Base Case: For \( n = 3 \), \( 3^{2} = 9 > 8 \). So, the statement is true for \( n = 3 \).

Inductive Step: Assume the statement is true for \( n = k \), i.e., \( 3^{k-1} > 2^k \).

For \( n = k + 1 \), \( 3^{(k+1)-1} = 3 \cdot 3^{k-1} > 3 \cdot 2^k = 3 \cdot 2^k > 2^{k+1} \).

Thus, the statement is true for \( n = k + 1 \).

By the principle of mathematical induction, the statement is true for all \( n \geq 3 \).

‚úÖ Answer: \(\boxed{3^{n-1} > 2^n}\)

Q3(iii). Prove that \( n! > 3^{n-1} \) for all integral values of \( n \geq 5 \).

Solution:

Base Case: For \( n = 5 \), \( 5! = 120 > 81 \). So, the statement is true for \( n = 5 \).

Inductive Step: Assume the statement is true for \( n = k \), i.e., \( k! > 3^{k-1} \).

For \( n = k + 1 \), \( (k+1)! = (k+1) \cdot k! > (k+1) \cdot 3^{k-1} \). Since \( k+1 \geq 6 \), \( (k+1) \cdot 3^{k-1} > 3^k \).

Thus, the statement is true for \( n = k + 1 \).

By the principle of mathematical induction, the statement is true for all \( n \geq 5 \).

‚úÖ Answer: \(\boxed{n! > 3^{n-1}}\)

Q5(i). Find the middle term in the expansion of \( (b - \frac{2c}{3})^{10} \).

Solution:

For \( n = 10 \) (even), the middle term is the \( \left(\frac{10}{2} + 1\right) \)th term, i.e., the 6th term.

The general term is \( T_{r+1} = \binom{10}{r} b^{10-r} \left(-\frac{2c}{3}\right)^r \).

For \( r = 5 \), \( T_6 = \binom{10}{5} b^5 \left(-\frac{2c}{3}\right)^5 = 252 \cdot b^5 \cdot \left(-\frac{32c^5}{243}\right) = -\frac{8064}{243} b^5 c^5 = -\frac{252}{81} b^5 c^5 \).

‚úÖ Answer: \(\boxed{-\frac{252}{81} b^5 c^5}\)

Q5(ii). Find the middle term in the expansion of \( (1 - \frac{1}{2}a^2)^{14} \).

Solution:

For \( n = 14 \) (even), the middle term is the \( \left(\frac{14}{2} + 1\right) \)th term, i.e., the 8th term.

The general term is \( T_{r+1} = \binom{14}{r} 1^{14-r} \left(-\frac{1}{2}a^2\right)^r \).

For \( r = 7 \), \( T_8 = \binom{14}{7} \left(-\frac{1}{2}a^2\right)^7 = 3432 \cdot \left(-\frac{1}{2}a^2\right)^7 = 3432 \cdot \left(-\frac{1}{128}a^{14}\right) = -\frac{3432}{128}a^{14} = -26.85a^{14} \).

‚úÖ Answer: \(\boxed{-26.85a^{14}}\)

Q5(iii). Find the middle term in the expansion of \( \left(\frac{a}{b} - \frac{b}{a}\right)^{18} \).

For \( r = 9 \), \( T_{10} = \binom{18}{9} \left(\frac{a}{b}\right)^9 \left(-\frac{b}{a}\right)^9 = \binom{18}{9} (-1)^9 = -48620 \).

Q5(iv). Find the middle term in the expansion of \( (a - \frac{1}{a})^{2n} \).

Solution:

For \( n = 2n \) (even), the middle term is the \( \left(\frac{2n}{2} + 1\right) \)th term, i.e., the \( (n + 1) \)th term.

For \( r = n \), \( T_{n+1} = \binom{2n}{n} a^{2n-n} \left(-\frac{1}{a}\right)^n = \binom{2n}{n} (-1)^n \).

Q6(i). Find the two middle terms of \( (x^3 + \frac{1}{x^2})^7 \).

Solution:

For \( n = 7 \) (odd), the two middle terms are the \( \left(\frac{7+1}{2}\right) \)th and \( \left(\frac{7+3}{2}\right) \)th terms, i.e., the 4th and 5th terms.

The general term is \( T_{r+1} = \binom{7}{r} (x^3)^{7-r} \left(\frac{1}{x^2}\right)^r \).

For \( r = 3 \), \( T_4 = \binom{7}{3} x^{12} \cdot \frac{1}{x^6} = 35x^6 \).

For \( r = 4 \), \( T_5 = \binom{7}{4} x^9 \cdot \frac{1}{x^8} = 35x \).

‚úÖ Answer: \(\boxed{35x^6}\) and \(\boxed{35x}\)

Q6(ii). Find the two middle terms of \( (2b - \frac{b^2}{4})^9 \).

For \( n = 9 \) (odd), the two middle terms are the \( \left(\frac{9+1}{2}\right) \)th and \( \left(\frac{9+3}{2}\right) \)th terms, i.e., the 5th and 6th terms.

The general term is \( T_{r+1} = \binom{9}{r} (2b)^{9-r} \left(-\frac{b^2}{4}\right)^r \).

For \( r = 4 \), \( T_5 = \binom{9}{4} (2b)^5 \left(-\frac{b^2}{4}\right)^4 = 126 \cdot 32b^5 \cdot \frac{b^8}{256} = \frac{4032}{256}b^{13} = 15.75b^{13} \).

For \( r = 5 \), \( T_6 = \binom{9}{5} (2b)^4 \left(-\frac{b^2}{4}\right)^5 = 126 \cdot 16b^4 \cdot \left(-\frac{b^{10}}{1024}\right) = -\frac{2016}{1024}b^{14} = -1.96875b^{14} \).

‚úÖ Answer: \(\boxed{15.75b^{13}}\) and \(\boxed{-1.96875b^{14}}\)

Q7(i). Find the term independent of \( x \) in \( (2x + \frac{1}{x^2})^9 \).

Solution:

The general term is \( T_{r+1} = \binom{9}{r} (2x)^{9-r} \left(\frac{1}{x^2}\right)^r = \binom{9}{r} 2^{9-r} x^{9-3r} \).

For the term independent of \( x \), set \( 9 - 3r = 0 \), so \( r = 3 \).

Thus, \( T_4 = \binom{9}{3} 2^{6} = 84 \cdot 64 = 5376 \).

‚úÖ Answer: \(\boxed{5376}\)

Q7(ii). Find the term independent of \( x \) in \( (2x + \frac{5}{x})^6 \).

For the term independent of \( x \), set \( 6 - 2r = 0 \), so \( r = 3 \).

Thus, \( T_4 = \binom{6}{3} 2^{3} 5^3 = 20 \cdot 8 \cdot 125 = 20000 \).

‚úÖ Answer: \(\boxed{20000}\)

Q8(i). Find the term involving \( x^6 \) in the expansion of \( (2x^3 - \frac{1}{x^2})^7 \).

‚úÖ Answer: \(\boxed{-560x^6}\)

Q8(iii). Find the coefficient of \( x \) in the expansion of \( (x^2 + \frac{a^2}{x})^5 \).

Solution:

Q9(i). Does the expansion of \( \left(\frac{3a}{2} - \frac{1}{3a^3}\right)^9 \) contain a term independent of \( a \)?

Q10. The coefficients of the fifth, sixth, and seventh terms of the expansion of \( (1+x)^n \) form an A.P. Find \( n \).
